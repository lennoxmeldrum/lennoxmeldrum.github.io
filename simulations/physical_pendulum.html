Physical Pendulum

The Physics of Pendulums

Physical vs. Simple Pendulum

A simple pendulum assumes all mass is concentrated at a single point at the end of a massless string. A physical pendulum is any real rigid body that oscillates about a fixed axis. Because its mass is distributed across its shape, we must account for its rotational inertia (I).

The Period of Oscillation

For small angles, the period T of a physical pendulum is given by:

T = 2π √(I / mgd)
  • I = Rotational inertia about the pivot
  • m = Total mass
  • d = Distance from the pivot to the Centre of Mass (CM)

The Parallel Axis Theorem

To find I about any pivot, we use the parallel axis theorem: I = Icm + md². For a uniform rod of length L, Icm = 1/12 mL². Notice how I changes as you move the pivot!

Fun Fact: The Minimum Period

As you move the pivot from the end towards the centre, the period actually decreases at first! It reaches a minimum when d = L/√12 ≈ 0.289L. Only after passing this point does the period start increasing towards infinity. Try finding the minimum period!

The Counter-Intuitive Centre

Try dragging the pivot exactly to the Centre of Mass (d = 0). The restoring torque (τ = −mgd sinθ) becomes zero because gravity pulls directly on the pivot, creating no leverage. However, the rotational inertia I is still 1/12 mL². With inertia but no restoring force, the period becomes infinite — it doesn't swing!

Equivalent Simple Pendulum

Every physical pendulum has a corresponding simple pendulum that swings with the exact same period. By equating their period formulas, we find the equivalent length:

Leq = I / md

AP Physics C: Mechanics (Topic 7.5)

Newton's Second Law (Rotational):
τ = Iα
Restoring Torque:
When displaced, gravity exerts a torque: τ = −mgd sinθ
Small-Angle Approximation:
For small θ, sinθ ≈ θ, so τ ≈ −mgdθ
Differential Equation for SHM:
Combining these gives −mgdθ = I (d²θ/dt²), which simplifies to d²θ/dt² = −ω²θ, the hallmark of Simple Harmonic Motion.
